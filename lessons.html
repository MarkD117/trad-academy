The Banjo

In traditional Irish music, the Irish Tenor Banjo originally gained popularity in the 1920s. From the American dance hall scene, where it was mostly utilized as an accompaniment instrument, it made its way to Ireland. Similar to a guitar, players would strum and play chords on the instrument. Irish musicians soon started to modify its use to play traditional song melodies instead.

The Mandolin

The mandolin dates back to the Middle Ages and is the ancestor of the lute. The Neapolitan or Italian style bowl-back mandolin, the arch-top mandolin, and the flat-back mandolin are the three main varieties of mandolins played today. The flat back variant is most frequently used in traditional Irish music, and it is frequently referred to as the "Irish mandolin". Over the years, mandolins have gained considerable popularity amongst Irish traditional performers.

One factor in their appeal, in addition to their bright tone and small stature, is the simplicity with which Irish fiddle and banjo players may switch from one to the other due to their similar left-hand fingering. The mandolin is for those who are looking for a sweet sounding instrument that synergizes well when playing with others. The Fiddle

One of the most widely used instruments in contemporary traditional Irish music is the violin, or fiddle as it is also known. Fiddles have existed since the Middle Ages, although, the modern fiddle did not become popular in traditional Irish music until the 17th century. The violin and the fiddle are the exact same instrument; however, some will argue that while a violin is a fiddle, not every fiddle is a violin. So what's the difference?

The difference is the style in which they are played. If it's is being used for classical, it is generally called a violin, when used for trad music, it's referred to as a fiddle. In trad music, a variety of ornamentation is used such as cuts, rolls and triplets. This style of playing is what gives it the name of the Irish fiddle. The Guitar

Although not a strictly traditional Irish instrument, the guitar is quite a popular choice when it comes to trad music. Generally, the guitar is used as an accompaniment to further develop the musicality of a performance. A good accompanist aims enhance the playing of others. This is done by understanding harmony, identifying harmionic changes, knowing the melody and struture of a tune, but, most importantly. the ability to listen to other musicians.

These are all skills that will be taught and perfected with the help of our tutors. In spite of the fact that the guitar is used for accompaniment, it can also be used to play melodies too! The Piano

From the 1920s onwards, the piano was used as an accompaniment instrument in recordings of traditional Irish music. While the piano was most likely introduced into the tradition much earlier, its role has evolved dramatically over time. Despite the presence of numerous highly recognized traditional Irish pianists, a distinctly Irish piano playing style has yet to emerge. As a result, there is a diverse range of interesting Irish piano playing styles to delight and inspire. The qualities of a good piano accompaniment are identical to that of the guitar.

While the guitar strums chords with tastful motifs, the piano typically uses a style known as vamping. in it's simplist form vamping is where chords are played alongside an alternating bassline. This highly rhythmic, chordal style of piano accompaniment has become widespread today in traditional Irish music. The vamping style has evolved throughout the years which lead to pianists incorporating elements of other musical genres such as playing melodies. Throughout your time with Trad Academy, you will learn all about these different styles of playing. Have a listen to the piano being played with both melody and accompaniment!
